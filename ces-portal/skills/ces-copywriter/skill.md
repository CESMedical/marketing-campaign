## Language and Punctuation Rules, Non-Negotiable

### British English always
Spell and punctuate in British English throughout. This is a Kent-based NHS and private healthcare provider writing for a UK audience.

Key spellings:
- "colour" not "color"
- "centre" not "center"
- "organisation" not "organization"
- "recognise" not "recognize"
- "licence" (noun) / "license" (verb)
- "practise" (verb) / "practice" (noun)
- "anaesthetic" not "anesthetic"
- "paediatric" not "pediatric"
- "catalogue" not "catalog"
- "theatre" not "theater"

### No em dashes, ever
Do not use em dashes (—) anywhere in any content: captions, slide labels, video titles, chapter timestamps, topic metadata, production notes or any other field. This rule has no exceptions. This is the single most common copywriting error in CES Medical content. Check every line before saving.

Mandatory replacements by context:

| Context | Wrong | Right |
|---|---|---|
| Slide labels | Slide 1 — Text | Slide 1: Text |
| Video timestamps | 0:00 to 0:30 — Hook | 0:00 to 0:30: Hook |
| Topic metadata | Topic: Title — subtitle | Topic: Title: subtitle |
| Post headings | P01 — Campaign launch | P01: Campaign launch |
| Parenthetical in body copy | consultants — all FRCOphth — see patients | consultants (all FRCOphth-qualified) see patients |
| Pause or emphasis | the result — cloudy vision | the result: cloudy vision |
| Introducing a clause | surgery — and it works | surgery, and it works |

If you find yourself typing — at any point, stop and rewrite using the alternatives above.

### No Oxford comma
British English convention. No serial comma before "and" or "or" in a list.
- Right: "cataract surgery, eyelid surgery and glaucoma management"
- Wrong: "cataract surgery, eyelid surgery, and glaucoma management"

### No outcome guarantee language
Never use "you will" constructions that imply a guaranteed clinical outcome. This is both an ASA compliance requirement and a brand voice requirement.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| "You will still need reading glasses" | "Most patients still need reading glasses" |
| "You will be seen by a consultant" | "Patients are seen by a consultant" |
| "You will see FRCOphth after their names" | "Our consultant surgeons carry FRCOphth" |
| "You will notice an improvement" | "Many patients notice an improvement" |
| "You will be back to daily activities" | "Most patients are back to daily activities" |

The correct pattern: "most patients", "many patients find", "patients typically", "in most cases." Never "you will" for a clinical outcome.

### No bold statements in healthcare
Never open a slide, post or headline with a provocative bold statement. This is a healthcare brand. Patients are anxious, not looking to be challenged. Lead with a question, a reassuring fact or a patient-centred observation instead.

## Platform Algorithm Timing Rules

These are non-negotiable posting times derived from verified 2026 algorithm data for each platform. Scheduling outside these windows reduces reach by up to 50%.

### LinkedIn
**Brand account:** Monday and Thursday at 8am
**Elion personal account:** Tuesday at 8am, overflow to Friday at 8am
**Consultant personal accounts:** Wednesday at 8am
**Why:** LinkedIn's algorithm prioritises content during professional browsing hours. Monday and Thursday are the highest-engagement days for B2B and healthcare professional content.

### Facebook
**Patient-facing posts:** Tuesday, Wednesday and Thursday at 8am
**Evening posts:** Tuesday, Wednesday and Thursday at 7pm (catches the 55-75 demographic browsing from home)
**Carer and family posts:** Saturday at 9am
**Why:** The 55-75 demographic scrolls Facebook in the morning, at lunchtime and in the evening. Saturday morning reaches carers and family members who are more relaxed and likely to share.

### Instagram
**Primary:** Wednesday at 9am
**Secondary:** Monday at 9am and Friday at 9am
**Why:** Wednesday consistently produces the highest engagement for healthcare accounts. Spreading to Monday and Friday prevents the Wednesday overcrowding that was identified in the campaign audit (31 posts on Wednesdays vs 3 on Mondays).

### YouTube
**Publish time:** 10am any day, Monday to Thursday preferred
**Why:** YouTube is search-driven, not feed-driven. Publish time matters less than metadata quality. One video per week minimum. Monday to Thursday uploads index faster because YouTube's crawlers are more active on weekdays.

### X
**Recycle posts:** Post on the same day as the primary platform post, within 2 hours of the original going live
**Why:** X is a recycling channel for CES Medical. Its primary function is extending reach from other platforms, not generating original reach.

### The golden hour rule (all platforms)
Within 60 minutes of any post going live, a team member must be available to respond to every comment. This is the single most important operational rule for reach across all platforms. A post that receives substantive engagement in the first hour will reach an audience 3 to 10 times larger than the same post with no early engagement.

### Day of week distribution rule
No more than 2 posts across all platforms on any single day. Posts on the same day must be on different platforms and go out at different times (minimum 1 hour apart).

## Brand Voice, The Rules

### Core principles

**1. Honest, not salesy.**
Never oversell. Trade-offs are always acknowledged. If a multifocal lens isn't right for someone, say so. If recovery takes longer than patients expect, say so. Patients trust CES because CES doesn't pretend surgery is simple, risk-free, or miraculous.

**2. Clinical authority without jargon.**
Consultants hold FRCOphth, Fellow of the Royal College of Ophthalmologists. Spell this out in full on first mention in any piece. After that, FRCOphth is acceptable shorthand. Never use credentials as decoration, always connect them to patient benefit ("your surgeon holds FRCOphth, the highest professional qualification for eye surgeons in the UK").

**3. Local and personal.**
Always reference Kent geography. Tunbridge Wells, Maidstone, Medway, Chatham, Headcorn, Northfleet. Never "near London." Never "the South East" as a lazy shorthand. The local rootedness is a commercial differentiator, a patient in Maidstone should feel like CES was built for them.

**4. Accessible to older audiences.**
The 65+ demographic is active on Facebook. Short sentences. Plain English. No jargon. Phone CTAs outperform links for this group. Write as if explaining to an intelligent, slightly anxious 68-year-old who has been putting off dealing with their eyes for two years, not because they don't care, but because they don't know what to expect.

**5. Reassuring about process.**
Patients delay, sometimes for years. The job of most CES content is not to sell surgery. It is to reduce the friction that keeps people from making the first enquiry. Make the next step feel small, safe, and sensible.

**6. Compliant at all times.**
See the compliance section. This is non-negotiable and sits above every other consideration.

## Critical Clinical Distinctions, Always Make These Clear

These are the distinctions that matter most to patients, and where muddled copy causes the most damage to trust.

### Lens types in cataract surgery
Never conflate. Always explain the difference when more than one type is mentioned.

| Lens | What it corrects | Likely outcome | Key honest trade-off |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Monofocal** | One focal distance, usually distance | Good distance vision; reading glasses still needed | Standard option. Well-established. NHS-comparable. |
| **Multifocal** | Multiple distances simultaneously | Many patients glasses-free | Not for everyone, some experience haloes or glare, especially at night |
| **EDOF** | Extended intermediate range | Reduced glasses dependence, fewer haloes than full multifocal | May still need reading glasses for fine print |
| **Toric** | Distance vision plus astigmatism correction | Good distance vision with reduced astigmatism | Precise positioning required; does not address reading vision |

**Multifocal ≠ varifocal.** Varifocals are glasses. Multifocal IOLs are surgical implants inside the eye. This distinction must be made explicit in any post that references both.

### Eyelid surgery, functional vs cosmetic
Always make this explicit. It affects clinical pathway, funding route, and patient expectation.

- **Functional ptosis repair / blepharoplasty**, medical indication (affects vision or visual field), often covered by private medical insurance
- **Cosmetic blepharoplasty**, elective, aesthetic outcome, self-funded
- Same surgeon, same skill, but different consent process, different funding route, different patient motivation

### NHS vs private pathway

**CES Medical's NHS position:**
CES Medical is an NHS-commissioned provider in Kent. It holds service contracts with Kent and Medway ICB (Integrated Care Board). This is a formal commissioning relationship — CES has gone through NHS procurement, quality assurance and service specification approval. This is not just a private practice that occasionally treats NHS patients. It is a commissioned NHS provider that also offers private services.

This distinction is critical in all copy. Never describe CES Medical as "a private group" without acknowledging its NHS-commissioned status. The correct description is "an NHS-commissioned ophthalmology provider offering both NHS and private services across Kent."

**What is the same across NHS and private:**
- Same consultants
- Same surgical standards
- Same clinical protocols
- Same facilities

**What is different for cataract patients:**
- Access speed: NHS waiting times vary, private offers prompt assessment and surgery scheduled around the patient
- Lens options: NHS provides standard monofocal lens, private offers the full range (monofocal, multifocal, EDOF, toric)
- Consultation time: private consultations are longer, allowing more time to discuss options

**What is different for other conditions:**
Private care at CES opens access to services and treatments that fall outside NHS commissioning entirely, including: dry eye IPL treatment, cosmetic blepharoplasty, elective oculoplastic procedures and premium diagnostics for elective conditions.

**What is never different:**
- Clinical quality
- Surgeon qualifications
- Safety standards

**Key rule for all copy:** Never position private as "better" quality care. Position it as faster access and broader scope. Private adds speed, choice and access to non-commissioned services. It does not add clinical quality.

**ICB relationship rule:** CES Medical must not be seen commercially exploiting NHS capacity concerns to drive private conversions. Any post that highlights NHS waiting times must also acknowledge CES's NHS pathway and offer both options to the reader. See P73 (Facebook) and P111 (Elion LinkedIn) for correct examples of this balance.

**Avoid in copy:**
- "We hold NHS contracts" — too informal. Use "We are an NHS-commissioned provider."
- "Private group" — implies CES is purely private. Incorrect.
- "Typically within two weeks" — hard timeline commitment, operational risk. Use "prompt assessment" or "surgery planned around your schedule" instead.

## Services, Clinical Knowledge Base

### Cataract surgery
Removal of the eye's natural clouded lens and replacement with an artificial intraocular lens (IOL). Day-case procedure under local anaesthetic. Typically 15–20 minutes per eye. Second eye usually treated separately, a few weeks apart.

**Patient profile:** Primarily 60–80. Often delayed out of fear or uncertainty. Common concerns: pain, going blind during surgery, general anaesthetic assumption (it is local), recovery time, and cost.

**Copy priorities:** Normalise the procedure. Reassure on process with specific detail. Explain lens choice clearly and honestly. Make the first enquiry feel low-stakes.

**Key technology:** Zeiss IOLMaster 700, cite by name, not as "advanced technology." Explain patient benefit: precise biometry means precise lens selection.

### Oculoplastic / eyelid surgery
Surgery on or around the eyelids. Covers functional repair (ptosis, ectropion, entropion) and cosmetic procedures (upper and lower blepharoplasty). Performed by oculoplastic surgeons, ophthalmologists with subspecialty training, not cosmetic surgeons.

**Key differentiator:** An oculoplastic surgeon understands ocular anatomy in a way a cosmetic surgeon does not. The eye is not just a skin problem. For eyelid surgery, specialist ophthalmological training matters, and this is a legitimate commercial differentiator that CES can state clearly.

**Patient profile:** 50–70 for functional presentations; 40–65 for cosmetic. Insurance may cover functional cases. Cosmetic is self-funded.

**Copy priorities:** Always distinguish functional from cosmetic. Explain the insurance pathway. Position the oculoplastic specialism as the clinically correct, and safer, choice.

### Glaucoma
A group of eye conditions that damage the optic nerve, usually associated with raised intraocular pressure. Often asymptomatic in early stages, detected at routine eye examinations. Management includes eye drops, laser (SLT, selective laser trabeculoplasty), and surgery.

**Copy priorities:** Emphasise the value of regular checks. Do not alarm, reassure that early detection means effective management. Use "diagnose and manage" rather than "treat" without qualification.

### Dry eye
A chronic condition where the eye does not produce sufficient tears or tears of sufficient quality. Ranges from mild irritation to significant impact on reading, screen work, and driving. CES offers IPL (Intense Pulsed Light) treatment alongside conventional management for patients who are not responding to drops alone.

**Copy priorities:** Name the IPL offering specifically, it signals a more advanced and proactive approach than drops. Validate the patient's experience, dry eye is frequently dismissed or undertreated by non-specialists.

### Diabetic retinopathy
Damage to the blood vessels of the retina caused by diabetes. A leading cause of preventable sight loss in working-age adults in the UK. Annual screening is essential for all diabetic patients.

**Copy priorities:** Urgency without alarm. Annual screening messaging. Position CES as the specialist complement to NHS diabetic eye screening, not a replacement for it.

### YAG laser capsulotomy
A short laser procedure, typically under ten minutes, to treat posterior capsule opacification (PCO). PCO causes cloudy vision that develops months or years after otherwise successful cataract surgery. Painless. Outpatient. Effect is usually immediate.

**Copy priorities:** Reassure patients who believe their cataract has returned, it has not. PCO is a routine complication with a simple, quick solution. Normalise the follow-up.

## Compliance, ASA/CAP Code Rules for Healthcare Copy

Non-negotiable. Any copy that violates these rules must be rewritten before publication.

### Absolute rules
1. **No unprovable superlatives.** "Best", "leading", "most advanced", "world-class" all require substantiation. If you cannot prove it with data, do not write it.
2. **No guaranteed outcomes.** Results vary by patient. Never write "you will", write "most patients find" or "many people find."
3. **No fear-based advertising.** Clinical urgency and manufactured fear are different things. Know the line.
4. **Testimonials must be genuine and representative.** Patient stories must reflect a plausible outcome, not an exceptional result presented as typical.
5. **Prices must be accurate and inclusive.** If a price is cited, it must include all mandatory charges, or exclusions must be stated clearly.
6. **Comparative claims require evidence.** "Faster than the NHS" is acceptable if data supports it. "Better than the NHS" is a quality claim that cannot be made.

### Yellow-flag phrases, rewrite or substantiate
- "Outcomes are consistent" → requires outcome data
- "Minimal risk" → all surgery carries risk; use "well-established safety profile" with supporting data
- "You may not need glasses" → acceptable; "you won't need glasses" → not acceptable
- "Our patients love us" → unsubstantiatable; use a specific, verified patient voice instead

## Patient Psychology, What Every CES Copywriter Must Understand

### Why patients delay
- Fear of surgery on their eyes, "what if something goes wrong"
- Uncertainty about cost and whether insurance covers it
- Not knowing whether they are "bad enough" to need it yet
- Associating cataracts with ageing, reluctance to acknowledge it
- Previous frustrating experiences with healthcare administration
- Not wanting to bother a stretched NHS system
- Lack of a trusted recommendation from someone they know

### What moves them forward
- A specific, clear explanation of the process, not vague reassurance
- Meeting a real consultant, name, face, credentials, personality
- Hearing from a patient who was exactly like them
- Understanding the full cost upfront with no surprises
- Being told the first step is small, "just a conversation, no commitment"
- Local availability, the clinic does not require a trip to London

### What puts them off
- Anything that feels salesy, pushy, or promotional
- Jargon that makes them feel uninformed or inferior
- Uncertainty about who is actually doing the surgery
- Stories that make complications feel common or likely
- Any process that feels complicated before they have even made contact

## What Good CES Copy Looks Like

### Strong opener, stops the scroll
> "Most people don't realise they have a cataract until their optician tells them, because it builds so gradually."

Works because: it starts with the patient's experience, not CES's offer. Creates immediate recognition.

### Strong clinical distinction
> "Varifocals are glasses. Multifocal lenses are implants placed inside your eye during cataract surgery. The confusion is common, and important to clear up before your consultation."

Works because: direct, educational, non-patronising. Validates the confusion before correcting it.

### Strong CTA, low friction
> "If you've been putting off a question about your eyes, this is your nudge. The first step is always a conversation, call us on 01732 755771."

Works because: acknowledges procrastination without judging it. Makes the next step feel minimal.

### What to avoid, weak opener
> "At CES Medical, we're proud to offer world-class cataract surgery to patients across Kent."

Fails because: starts with CES, not the patient. "World-class" is a compliance risk. "Proud to offer" is corporate filler with no patient benefit.
